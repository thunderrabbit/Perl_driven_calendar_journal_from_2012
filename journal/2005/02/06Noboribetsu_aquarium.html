2300 yen to enter; a tad high considering the limited number of exhibits, but it wasn't too crowded, so we had a generally decent experience. Into the main castle where we were greeted with a large vertical aquarium with several varieties of fish milling around.

Riding two escalators to the third level, we overlook two large tanks with ray looking things in one and shark looking things in the other. Third floor features tanks with starfish, rays, those round crabs that look prehistoric and some others, all of which we were allowed to touch.

(( In the Houston zoo, there is an exhibit where we can touch the starfish. Before introducing our hand and stinky human oils in the water, we are asked by the docent carefully monitoring the animals to wash (rinse) our hands. I never really thought a quick rinse would do much good, but perhaps it does a lot, and probably it is better than nothing. ))

There is a sink available for us to wash our hands, after we touch the animals. My sensibilities were offended by this seeming backwardness.

Only one animal looked suprising to me: flat underwater frogs. They truly looked like squashed flat toads (no protruding guts), except they were living underwater. Not that they were moving, but I guess they were alive. I forgot to get a picture of them, though.

We scoped the dolphin show and I thought a lot of Erika in Connecticut; I wonder if she leads shows; I think she'd be great at it: tall, genki, and beautiful.

Zipped through the reptile house and I photographed some of the turtles.

Then we scoped the sea lion show, which was far more entertaining than the dolphins. The dolphins are stronger and can jump way higher, but the sea lions are way way more dexterious.

The sea lions played basketball and volleyball, walked on their front flippers, did flips and did awesome spins while balancing a ball on their noses.

One of the funniest thing was after they walked across stage on their front flippers. The girl had them balance up on their flippers and then follow her as she walked backwards to the other side of the stage. She was obviously watching them during their performance. We all clapped wildly cause oh my god their flippers must be strong. They rested for a moment as we clapped and then she was talking to the seals, [oh, they liked it! Let's show them again.] The seals nodded in agreement. So she was like, [okay, UP!] and they balanced on their flippers again. She turned around and led them to the other side of the stage. As she turned around, they immediately dropped onto their bodies (no flipper balancing) and slid behind her.

She turned around and discovered they hadn't walked, and they all dramatically folded their necks over sideways and hid their faces behind their flippers. So funny!!

I'm really happy that I got some of their coolest tricks on video.
