Dear Rob,

First let me say that I sobbed when I read your website (was it just 
yesterday? yes) and I have thought about you a lot. As your mom, your pain manifests 
in me physically even when I know I have no power to fix the problem. You are 
harboring a lot of anger, it seems to me, and trying to stuff it down somewhere 
inside yourself and it won't work indefinitely. The pain is coming out 
internally, at any rate, and also affecting people who love you.

Maggie is very smart in her distinction between comfort and healing. The 
mantra I have heard over and over is that "the only way out is through," referring 
to inner pain. You just have to go through it to get to the other side. The 
year your dad moved out and the following year, after the divorce was final, 
were the most painful years in my life, at least to that point. At times I felt 
as though I would just die. Not by suicide, but by not being able to bear the 
pain. What kept me going was the knowledge that somehow I would get through 
it. Countless people had lived through divorce--it's not fatal by itself! So I 
would sit in the sun and weep, trying to recharge my batteries through natural 
energy.

I also relied on friends who were willing to listen. Mostly, though, I got 
myself to a therapist. Friendship can be sorely tested when a lament goes on and 
on practically endlessly. I also started taking an anti-depressant. It wasn't 
the newfangled prozac or anything like that, but an old, tried and true 
variety that was available as a generic. And it helped immensely. It didn't 
eliminate the pain or the problem, but allowed me to function on a daily basis while 
I continued meeting with my therapist weekly.

Clearly you have a problem with relationships, especially with women. My 
impression is that you are looking for a perfect partner even though (a) there is 
no such person and (b) you are not perfect, either. I think it would be hard 
to live with a perfect person. How would I look next to that? Relationships 
require a certain amount of compromise. Experience in my first marriage, therapy 
with a lot of self-reflection, and occasional dating helped me figure out the 
most important things in a committed relationship. Jon met most of these 
criteria. He is far from perfect, but we do not expect that in one another. We have 
negotiated the things that irritate us the most, and learned to live with 
things that are not worth that effort.

It seems to me that you would benefit from therapy. I don't know if a male or 
female would work better for you, but competence is far more important than 
gender. My guess is that you would do well to find a male therapist. I don't 
know if such a person can be found in Tokyo or Yokohama, but it's quite 
possible. Probably an American would be better for you. Conceivably, if you have had a 
therapist in the States, he or she might be willing to correspond via email. 
Another valuable resource is columnist Carolyn Hax, who has a chat room as 
well as a syndicated advice column and I really think she's good at getting to 
the core of a problem and gives good advice. I will look for her address so you 
can just read some of her stuff.

I would also send you a book I like: Getting the Love You Want. It's a stupid 
title and I avoided it for a long time, but author Harville Hendrix makes a 
lot of sense. Actually I just pulled it off the shelf and the subtitle is A 
Guide for Couples, but I still think it's a good resource. He talks about 
childhood wounds and how they affect your relationships. It has a chapter on anger, 
too.

want women who meet your ideal. Instead, you need to think about how your
